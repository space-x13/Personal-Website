Biography

Albert Einstein (1879-1955) was a German-born theoritical physicist who is widely regarded as one of the greatest scientists of all time. He is best known for developing the theory of relativity, which relovutionized our understanding of spaceüöÄ, time‚è≤, and gravityüåç. His equation (E=mc^2) demonstrated the equivalence of mass and energy. In 1921, he recieved the Noble Prize in Physics for his explanation of the photoelectric effects. Einstein's work profoundly impacted scientific thought and technology, establishing him as one of history's most influential scientists.

Photoelectric Effect (1905)

Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light is composed of particles called photons. This work provided crucial evidence for quantum theory and won him the Noble Prize in Physics in 1921.

Einstein Field Equations (1915)

As part of general relativity, Einstein formulated the Einstein field equations, which describe how matter and energy in the universeüåå influence the curvature of spacetime.

Brownian Motion (1905)

Einstein provided a mathematical explanation for Brownian motion, the random movement of particles suspended in a fluid. This work provided empirical evidence for the existence of atoms and molecules.

Bose-Einstein Condensate (1924)

Collaborating with Indian physicist Satyendra Nath Bose, Einstein predicted a new state of matter known as the Bose-Einstein condensate. This state occurs at temperature close to absolute zero, where a group of bosons occupies the same quantum state.

Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen paradox challenged the completeness of quantum mechanics, arguing that the theory could not provide a full description of physical reality.
This work led to significant debates and further advancement in quantum theory.
